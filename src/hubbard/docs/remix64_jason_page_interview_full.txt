Remix64 Interview with Jason Page
Source: https://remix64.com/interviews/interview-jason-page.html
Interviewer: Neil Carr
Date: 27 May 2002

Subject: Jason Page (Handle: Jay)
Born: 1972
Nationality: British

NOTE: Jason Page is NOT 4mat (Matt Simmonds). Jason Page worked at Graftgold
and Sony. He created RobTracker (a C64 music player based on Hubbard's Digi
routine) as a separate project, distinct from 4mat's GoatTracker converter.

=============================================================================
FULL INTERVIEW TEXT
=============================================================================

Neil: What other c64 musicians did you like?

Jason: "Well, it has to be the old favourites, doesn't it? Rob Hubbard, Martin
Galway, Maniacs Of noise (especially Jeroen Tel). Ratt&Ben did some good stuff
too. Probably would have sounded even better if it was using MON's audio
routines!"

Neil: What are your favourite Sids?

Jason: "Sanxion, Stormlord, Ocean loading music and Rambo spring to mind."

Neil: Ok lets start from the very beginning, What initial steps did you take
into becoming a musician on the c64?

Jason: "I bought a C64, and heard Thing On A Spring. That made me realise that
this machine was something a bit special. I had always been interested in the
audio side of things, and as a programmer, wanted to know how someone could
make 3 channels of SID sound so good. Programs like Sound Monitor also helped
a lot, as it gave away a lot of techniques – such as phasing a square wave and
so on."

Neil: How did you get your first opportunity in creating music for c64 games?

Jason: "Graftgold was based in my hometown of Witham in Essex. I was lucky
enough to get a job there when I was 16. I started as a trainee programmer,
and then started doing the music for their games also. Steve Turner used to do
the audio for their titles before this. He's a very good guitar player, by the
way. I think the first track I worked on was a game called Orion, written by
Gary Foreman."

Neil: How did it feel when you saw your name on the credits for a game?

Jason: "It felt great, actually! It was that event that you always wanted to
happen. Before working at Graftgold, I had been programming on the C64 for
many years. Putting demos and music on Compunet and so on. And through all of
this time, I just wanted my name in a game. It was like, whatever happened
from that point, at least I could say that I was there once."

Neil: What did you use to create your music on the c64?

Jason: "I started with Sound Monitor and then Rock Monitor. I moved onto using
that Maniacs Of Noise rip-off thing (can't remember the name). At Graftgold,
I used the routine which Steve Turner wrote, and at this point, I was also
writing my own routine in my spare time. Oh, and what was that other one which
Demon (from Psy&Mat demo days) used? Er..ElectroSound! Yeah, I used that for
a while too. The code for running their audio data had bugs in it though."

Neil: What was your likes/dislikes regarding the sid chip?

Jason: "The Filters were a little unstable, weren't they? Only having 3
channels wasn't really an issue then, as its not as if you had a choice between
that or something far better at the time. The thing is, that the SID chip
doesn't sound very good at all unless the audio program does clever things
with it. For example, the MON drum sounds were far better than anything else,
in my opinion. This was due to them having lookup tables of pitch and waveform
data, so the program just ran through this list very quickly. Other people were
just flipping between a triangle waveform and white noise, which was nowhere
near as good as the MON way of doing things. But that was the best thing about
the SID. You could do so much with it. And you could tell if a tune was by Rob
Hubbard or Martin Galway just by the sound of it. Not the actual notes, but
how their audio routines were playing the SID."

Neil: In your opinion what do you think was your best c64 tune?

Jason: "Probably one of my Compunet demo tunes actually, but I can't remember
any of them now. I used to just give them names like the data or time that I
finished the tune. I think I wrote one called 1010pm and another called 1105
?! I guess my favourite game tune was Head The Ball or the conversion of Super
Offroad racer."

Neil: Did you create music for any other platform apart from the c64?

Jason: "Well, I didn't then. But since the C64, I've written music for Amiga,
CD32, ST, Megadrive, SNES, PC, PS1 and PS2."

Neil: Did you work in-house or freelance, and what would you say are the
pro's and con's?

Jason: "I worked in-house at Graftgold. But remember that I was also
programming games at this point also, so it wasn't just the audio that I was
taking care of. I have sort of worked freelance since the Graftgold days. The
pro's are that you can work whenever you like. If you get inspired at 4am,
that's fine. But, that's also one of the cons. You can't just get away from
your work, and its easy to forget what else there is in life. Also, obviously
with freelance work, you have to keep looking for more work, and if a company
can get someone else to do it cheaper, you either don't bother with it, or
drop your price to get the work."

Neil: Was the money you made from making c64 music enough to live on, and did
you consider your work more as a hobby or a real job?

Jason: "Well, at the time I was still living with my parents. I don't think I
could have afforded to move out though! Nobody really did it for the money. It
was just for the fun of it. Because there were real hours and deadlines to work
for, it wasn't like a hobby in that aspect. But it did (and still does) feel
that I'm getting paid for something I used to do for nothing. Which is nice."

Neil: Were you ever treated unfairly by a games company?

Jason: "Not really. No more than anyone else. The thing with any creative
industry is that its full of creative, emotional people. Who always think
they're getting treated unfairly! This industry is also very fickle.
Basically, don't trust anyone. And always keep as many emails as possible to
cover your back, two years later."

Neil: Who would you credit most of all for your introduction to making c64
music, and why?

Jason: "Rob Hubbard, as it was his tunes that I heard before anyone else. Note
that its also his fault (!) that I'm still doing game music 15 years later!"

Neil: If there was a tune that you would wish to call your own, what would it
be and why?

Jason: "Sanxion, because it's a great track. Who else used to stop the tape
from loading the title screen, just to hear the track all the way through?!
Ah...Just me..I'll shut up then."

Neil: Of late we have seen a few remixes of c64 tunes on rko by yourself, why
did you decide to make these remixes?

Jason: "I've always wanted to do some, but only now have I had the time. Then,
last year (2001), I went along to the C64 Bits Live audio event in London. I
realised how many people still cared about the old C64 and Amiga music. I was
amazed that people knew both of my old music and myself. So I sort of felt
obliged to do something else for them."

Neil: What equipment/software do you use to create these remixes?

Jason: "Cubase VST running on a MacG3.
SampleCell2
Novation SuperNova
Roland JV1080
Emu Orbit
Kurzweil K2000
Mackie 24-8-2 desk"

Neil: What other arrangers do you like?

Jason: "Lots! Chris Abbott stands out as one of the best."

Neil: The remixes you have done have been based on other artists, have you
considered re-working your own c64 tunes?

Jason: "Yes. It may happen, but it feels a bit wrong somehow. It would be more
of a rewrite than a remix in this case. I'd rather let other people do remixes
of my music, and I'll remix theirs. Saying that, I may be doing a Uridium2
remix at some point (Although this was an Amiga tune). Mainly due to the fact
that I've lost the DAT tape which had the unreleased CD32 version of the title
music. So I'm itching to rewrite it again!"

Neil: What does Jason Page do now?

Jason: "He sits at his desk at work, filling in questionnaires instead of
doing what he gets paid for! Seriously, I'm the audio manager for Sony
Computer Entertainment Europe. I'm still programming and writing music for the
PlayStation and PS2."

Neil: Lastly, what would you like to say to the scene?

Jason: "Thanks to the people who email me saying nice things about my old work.
And I'm glad (and somewhat surprised) that so many people still care for the
SID. It's a great."

TECHNICAL OBSERVATIONS (extracted from interview)
=============================================================================

1. MON drum technique (Maniacs of Noise):
   Jason describes MON drums as superior because they use "lookup tables of
   pitch and waveform data, so the program just ran through this list very
   quickly." The contrast is with simpler approaches that only flipped between
   triangle and noise waveforms. This is the GT2/GoatTracker-style drum table
   approach: rapid cycling through a pre-defined sequence of register values
   for each drum frame.

2. Player identity by sound, not notes:
   "You could tell if a tune was by Rob Hubbard or Martin Galway just by the
   sound of it. Not the actual notes, but how their audio routines were playing
   the SID." — This confirms that the driver architecture (not just the notes
   or instruments) imparts a characteristic sound signature.

3. Jason composed using multiple drivers:
   - Sound Monitor (early)
   - Rock Monitor
   - "Maniacs Of Noise rip-off thing" (Jochen Hippel's driver? or a direct MON
     variant -- name not recalled)
   - Steve Turner's Graftgold routine
   - His own custom routine (written in spare time)
   - ElectroSound (Demon/Psy&Mat era) -- noted as having bugs in the audio
     data code

4. RobTracker context:
   The interview (2002) predates RobTracker. RobTracker was released later as
   part of Project Hubbard. It is based on Hubbard's Digi routine ($D418
   volume-register sample playback). See csdb_robtracker_jason_page.md for
   technical details.
